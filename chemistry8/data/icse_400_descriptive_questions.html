Q1
Olympiad / NEET Foundation Why/How Analytical
1. Matter
Why does the addition of a non-volatile solute like sodium chloride to water increase its boiling point but lower its freezing point? Explain using the kinetic molecular theory.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

According to the kinetic theory, a non-volatile solute occupies space on the liquid's surface, reducing the number of solvent molecules escaping into the vapor phase. Thus, a higher temperature is required to raise its vapor pressure to match atmospheric pressure (boiling point elevation). Conversely, solute particles disrupt the orderly organization of solvent molecules into a solid crystal lattice, requiring a lower temperature to freeze the solution (freezing point depression).

Q2
Hard Why/How Analytical
1. Matter
Why does ice float on water, and how does this anomalous density pattern support aquatic life during extreme winters?
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Below 4°C, water molecules form a cage-like hexagonal structure due to hydrogen bonding, which increases the intermolecular space and decreases the density, making ice less dense than liquid water. Ice therefore floats, acting as an insulating layer that prevents the water body from freezing completely to the bottom, allowing aquatic organisms to survive in the liquid water below.

Q3
Hard Why/How Analytical
1. Matter
Why is the latent heat of vaporization of water (2260 kJ/kg) significantly higher than its latent heat of fusion (334 kJ/kg)? Explain at the molecular level.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

During fusion (melting), energy is only needed to partially overcome and loosen the intermolecular attractive forces (hydrogen bonds) to allow molecules to flow. During vaporization, however, all intermolecular forces must be completely broken to separate the molecules into the gaseous state, which requires vastly more energy.

Q4
Olympiad / NEET Foundation Why/How Analytical
1. Matter
How does Graham's law of diffusion explain why a balloon filled with helium deflates faster than one filled with nitrogen or air under the same conditions?
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Graham's Law states that the rate of diffusion of a gas is inversely proportional to the square root of its molecular mass (r ∝ 1/√M). Since helium has a molecular mass of 4 u and nitrogen is 28 u, helium molecules move much faster and escape through the microscopic pores of the balloon material at a much higher rate.

Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

In liquids, increasing temperature increases kinetic energy, which overcomes the cohesive forces between molecules, reducing viscosity. Raising the temperature increases molecular speeds, leading to more frequent collisions, which increases the viscosity of gases.

Why does a gas exert pressure on the walls of its container, and how does increasing the volume of the container at constant temperature decrease this pressure?
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Gas pressure is caused by the continuous, random collisions of gas molecules against the container walls. According to Boyle's Law, when volume increases at constant temperature, the same number of molecules have a larger space to move in. This reduces the number of molecular collisions per unit area of the walls, decreasing the pressure.

Q12
Olympiad / NEET Foundation Why/How Analytical
1. Matter
Why does diffusion of gases occur faster than liquids, and why is diffusion of solids practically non-existent?
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

In gases, intermolecular spaces are extremely large and intermolecular forces are negligible, allowing molecules to move freely at high speeds. In liquids, molecules are closer together with moderate forces, restricting movement. In solids, molecules are held rigidly in fixed positions by extremely strong forces, permitting only vibrational motion about their mean positions, preventing diffusion.

Why do heavy iron ships float on water while a small iron nail sinks immediately? Explain using physical concepts.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

According to Archimedes' Principle, an object floats if it displaces a weight of water equal to its own weight. Why is the dissolving of anhydrous copper sulphate in water highly exothermic, while dissolving common salt (NaCl) is slightly endothermic?

What are 'Fossil Fuels'? Name the three major fossil fuels and discuss their environmental impacts.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Fossil fuels are hydrocarbon-rich fuels formed from dead organic remains of plants and animals buried under the earth over millions of years. Examples: Coal, Petroleum, and Natural Gas. Explain how the 'Carbon Cycle' maintains a stable level of carbon dioxide in nature. List two geological processes involved.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

The carbon cycle circulates carbon between the atmosphere, oceans, biosphere, and geosphere. Photosynthesis removes CO2, while respiration and combustion release it. Geological processes: 1. Weathering of silicate rocks (removing CO2). 2. Volcanic eruptions (releasing trapped CO2).

Describe the chemical structure of 'Graphene'. Why is it considered a revolutionary nanomaterial in modern science?
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Graphene is a single, flat, two-dimensional sheet of carbon atoms sp2 hybridized in a honeycomb hexagonal lattice. It is revolutionary because it is extremely thin (one atom thick), 200 times stronger than steel, highly flexible, and conducts electricity and heat better than copper.

9. Carbon and its Compounds
What is 'Wood Charcoal'? How is it prepared, and explain its reducing property when heated with metallic oxides.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Wood charcoal is an amorphous allotrope of carbon prepared by heating wood in a limited supply of air. It is a powerful reducing agent. When heated with metal oxides, it reduces them to elemental metals: PbO + C → Pb + CO.

What is 'Lamp Black'? How is it manufactured, and state two of its industrial applications.
Model Answer / Solution Explainer:

Lamp black is a fine black amorphous carbon powder. It is manufactured by burning carbon-rich substances (like kerosene, turpentine oil) in a limited supply of air and collecting the soot on metal plates. Applications: 1. Making black printing inks and shoe polishes. 2. As a filler in rubber tyres to improve strength.
