Why is the latent heat of vaporization of water (2260 kJ/kg) significantly higher than its latent heat of fusion (334 kJ/kg)? Explain at the molecular level.
First Principles Breakdown:

Enthalpy of phase transitions relies on breaking intermolecular bonds. $\Delta H_{fus}$ (334 kJ/kg) only requires breaking ~15% of the hydrogen bonds to allow translational fluidity (melting). Conversely, $\Delta H_{vap}$ (2260 kJ/kg) demands the complete annihilation of 100% of hydrogen bonds to separate molecules infinitely, plus significant expansion work ($P\Delta V$) against 1 atm of atmospheric pressure. Thus, $E_{vap} \gg E_{fus}$.

How does Graham's law of diffusion explain why a balloon filled with helium deflates faster than one filled with nitrogen or air under the same conditions?
Glassbox Logic:

At constant Temperature, average kinetic energy is invariant: $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Therefore, molecular velocity $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. Graham's Law of Effusion applies this: $r \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. Comparing Helium ($M=4$) to Nitrogen ($M=28$), the ratio $r_{He}/r_{N_2} = \sqrt{28/4} = \sqrt{7} \approx 2.64$. Helium atoms travel 2.64 times faster, resulting in a proportionally higher collision frequency with the balloon's micro-pores, accelerating deflation.
