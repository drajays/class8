How does galvanization prevent iron from rusting? Why does the zinc coating continue protecting even if scratched?
First Principles Breakdown:

Galvanization applies a physical and electrochemical Zinc shield. Zinc possesses a significantly more negative Standard Reduction Potential ($E^\circ_{Zn^{2+}/Zn} = -0.76\text{V}$) compared to Iron ($E^\circ_{Fe^{2+}/Fe} = -0.44\text{V}$). If a scratch exposes the Iron, a spontaneous localized Galvanic cell forms. Because Zinc is the stronger reducing agent, it acts as a "sacrificial anode." It preferentially strips its own electrons and pumps them into the Iron lattice. This constant electron surplus artificially converts the Iron into a protected cathode, making it thermodynamically impossible for Iron to undergo oxidation ($Fe \to Fe^{2+}$) as long as trace Zinc remains.

Why is respiration in living cells classified as a slow combustion chemical change? Explain how it is exothermic.
Glassbox Logic:

Both combustion and respiration share the identical macroscopic redox equation: $C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \to 6CO_2 + 6H_2O$. However, open combustion violently shatters all bonds simultaneously, dumping the entire Enthalpy ($\Delta H$) as unregulated sensible heat. Respiration is a highly controlled, multi-step enzymatic cascade (Glycolysis, Krebs Cycle, ETC). Enzymes lower precise Activation Energies ($E_a$) to bleed this potential energy out incrementally. This exothermic energy is systematically trapped by phosphorylating ADP into ATP ($\Delta G \approx -30.5\text{ kJ/mol}$), making it a cold, slow, isothermal "combustion" sustaining biological thermodynamics.

Why does a piece of bread taste sweet after being chewed for some time? What chemical change occurs in the mouth?
First Principles Breakdown:

Bread is predominantly composed of starch (amylose and amylopectin), which are massive, tasteless polysaccharide biopolymers. Mastication (chewing) physically maximizes the substrate's surface area. Human saliva introduces the enzyme Salivary Amylase (Ptyalin). This enzyme acts as a highly specific biological catalyst, attacking and hydrolyzing the rigid $\alpha-1,4$ glycosidic bonds linking the starch monomers. It chemically cleaves the heavy polymer into discrete, low-molecular-weight disaccharides (Maltose). Maltose uniquely fits the stereochemical receptors on the tongue's taste buds, triggering the neurological sensation of sweetness.

How does the 'Law of Conservation of Mass' hold true in the combustion of wood when the resulting ash weighs much less than the original wood?
Glassbox Logic:

Lavoisier's Law dictates that $\Sigma m_{reactants} = \Sigma m_{products}$. The perceived mass loss is a thermodynamic illusion caused by observing an open system. Wood is heavily composed of Cellulose ($C, H, O$). During the violent exothermic oxidation, these atoms are converted into high-vapor-pressure gases ($CO_2$ and $H_2O$ vapor) that physically escape into the boundless atmosphere, leaving only non-volatile mineral traces (ash). If this exact reaction were executed inside a hermetically sealed Bomb Calorimeter, the aggregate mass of the solid ash plus the trapped effluent gases would precisely equal the initial mass of the wood plus the consumed atmospheric oxygen.
