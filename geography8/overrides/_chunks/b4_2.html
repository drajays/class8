7. India: Geographical Features
UPSC Foundation: Why are the Western Coastal Plains narrow and the Eastern Coastal Plains broad? Explain geological reasons.
First Principles Breakdown:

This asymmetry is rooted in tectonic faulting and the tilt of the Deccan plate.
Western Coastal Plains: This is a Submerged Coast. Millions of years ago, the western flank of the peninsula sheared off and collapsed into the Arabian Sea. The ocean rushed right up to the sheer cliff face of the Western Ghats, leaving only a razor-thin, deep-water coastal strip, ideal for natural harbors but devoid of massive deltas because fast, short rivers (Narmada/Tapi) wash their silt directly into deep water.
Eastern Coastal Plains: This is an Emergent Coast. Because the entire Deccan Plateau tilts eastward, all major rivers (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna) flow east. Over millennia, these rivers have dumped billions of tons of sediment onto the shallow continental shelf, engaging in massive "Deltaic Progradation" that physically builds land out into the sea, forging a broad, flat, hyper-fertile coastal plain.

Glassbox Logic:

East-Flowing (into the Bay of Bengal): Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri. These rivers follow the natural eastward tilt of the plateau, forming massive, fertile deltas.

7. India: Geographical Features
Why do most peninsular rivers flow eastwards into the Bay of Bengal?
First Principles Breakdown:

Water strictly obeys the hydraulic gradient established by underlying geology. During the violent tectonic separation of India from Madagascar/Africa, the western edge of the Deccan shield was severely uplifted, forging the towering Western Ghats. This tectonic pivoting created an absolute, irreversible topological tilt across the entire Peninsular Plateau, sloping uniformly from West to East. Consequently, any precipitation falling on the plateau is mechanically forced by gravity to flow eastward down this gradient, ultimately terminating in the Bay of Bengal.

MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
How is the Thar Desert formed? Why does it lie in western Rajasthan?
Glassbox Logic:

The Thar Desert is an artifact of atmospheric alignment and mountain geometry.
As the moisture-laden South-West Monsoon (Arabian Sea branch) sweeps over Gujarat into Rajasthan, it encounters the ancient Aravalli Range. Crucially, the Aravallis run exactly parallel to the trajectory of these winds. Because the mountains do not block the wind perpendicularly, there is zero Orographic Uplift. The winds blow straight past without cooling or dropping their moisture. Furthermore, the immense summer heat of the Thar bakes the air, increasing its moisture-holding capacity and actively suppressing any localized cloud formation, locking the region into eternal aridity.

Q241
HardWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
Why are the Andaman & Nicobar Islands volcanic while Lakshadweep Islands are coral atolls?
Glassbox Logic:

This represents two totally divergent modes of island genesis.
Andaman & Nicobar: Tectonic origin. They are the emergent, exposed peaks of a massive submarine mountain range (an extension of Myanmar's Arakan Yoma). They sit directly atop an active subduction zone where the Indian plate dives under the Burma plate, generating extreme magma plumes and active stratovolcanoes (e.g., Barren Island).
Lakshadweep: Biological origin. Built upon sunken, dead volcanic seamounts. Millions of microscopic coral polyps extracted $CaCO_3$ from the shallow, sunlit seawater over millennia, synthesizing a colossal, circular limestone exoskeleton (atolls). There is no active tectonic uplift; the islands are purely biological calcium structures.

Q242
MediumConceptual Descriptive
7. India: Geographical Features
Explain the factors influencing India's climate – latitude, Himalayas, monsoon winds, distance from sea, relief.
First Principles Breakdown:

India's macro-climate is a multi-variable equation:
1. Latitude: The Tropic of Cancer bisects the nation, subjecting the south to relentless tropical heat, while the north experiences extreme subtropical continentality.
2. The Himalayas: Acts as an absolute thermal barricade, physically blocking lethal sub-zero Siberian winds, ensuring India's winters remain anomalously warm.
3. Monsoon Winds: Periodic pressure reversals dictate the entire hydraulic calendar, delivering $80\%$ of annual rain in just 4 months.
4. Distance from Sea: Coastal zones enjoy stable, moderated microclimates (high specific heat of water), while deep inland zones (Delhi) suffer brutal thermal extremes.
5. Relief: The altitude of the Western Ghats intercepts moisture, causing immense orographic rainfall on the windward side and devastating rain-shadow droughts on the leeward Deccan plateau.

Q243
MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
How does the monsoon mechanism work in India? Explain onset, burst, break and withdrawal.
Glassbox Logic:

The monsoon is a colossal thermodynamic pressure oscillation.
Onset & Burst: In June, the Tibetan plateau super-heats, forming an intense Low-Pressure void. The Sub-Tropical Jet Stream suddenly shifts northward. High-pressure air over the Southern Indian Ocean crosses the equator, violently striking the Kerala coast as the moisture-laden SW Monsoon. The sudden, violent commencement of torrential thunderstorms is the 'Burst'.
Break: The ITCZ (monsoon trough) fluctuates. If it shifts violently toward the Himalayan foothills, rainfall over the central plains stops entirely for weeks—a hazardous "Break in Monsoon."
Withdrawal: By October, the sun retreats south. The land cools, establishing High Pressure. The monsoon gracefully retreats, reversing wind direction (NE Monsoon) and clearing the skies.

What is meant by 'burst of monsoon'?
First Principles Breakdown:

The 'Burst of Monsoon' refers to the abrupt, highly dramatic transition in weather dynamics over the Indian peninsula during the first week of June. Following months of agonizing, scorching summer heat, the South-West Monsoon winds finally cross the coastline. The atmospheric instability triggers violent, continuous thunderstorms and relentless lightning, radically dropping ambient temperatures and flooding the parched soil almost overnight. It signifies the formal ignition of the agricultural year.

Q245
MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
Why does Cherrapunji / Mawsynram receive the highest rainfall in the world?
Glassbox Logic:

This is a flawless example of localized Orographic Funneling. Mawsynram is situated in Meghalaya, precisely at the crest of the Khasi Hills. Crucially, the Garo, Khasi, and Jaintia hills are aligned in a rigid, funnel-like configuration that opens directly southward toward the Bay of Bengal. As the hyper-moist SW monsoon winds sweep inland, they are caught in this geographic funnel and violently compressed. With nowhere to go but straight up, the air expands, cools adiabatically, and unleashes near-continuous, catastrophic precipitation ($>11,000 \text{ mm/year}$) strictly upon this tiny highland plateau.
