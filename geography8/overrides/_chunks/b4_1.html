7. India: Geographical Features
Why is India called a subcontinent?
Glassbox Logic:

A subcontinent is an immense, distinct, and relatively self-contained landmass forming a subdivision of a continent. India earns this title due to Geological Isolation. The formidable tectonic barricades of the Himalayas, Hindu Kush, and Arakan Yoma physically sever the region from the rest of Eurasia. This absolute geographical isolation allowed the Indian landmass to incubate its own entirely unique meteorological engine (the Monsoons) and foster deeply distinct endemic biodiversity, cultures, and linguistic matrices immune from broader Asian dilution.

How does India's central location between East and West Asia help in trade?
Glassbox Logic:

India sits exactly at the apex of the Indian Ocean, dominating the trans-oceanic routes connecting industrialized Europe to booming East Asia. Q231
MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
How were the Himalayas formed? Name its three parallel ranges.
Glassbox Logic:

The Himalayas are an artifact of a catastrophic Continental Convergent Boundary. Approximately 50 million years ago, the dense Indo-Australian plate violently rammed into the colossal Eurasian plate. The sedimentary rock of the intervening Tethys Sea was caught in the vice. Unable to subduct due to its low density, this sediment was aggressively compressed, folded, and thrust vertically into the stratosphere.
The continuous crumpling formed three parallel longitudinal tiers:
1. Himadri (Greater Himalayas - the highest, continuous glacial core).
2. Himachal (Lesser Himalayas - highly rugged, famous for hill stations).
3. Shiwalik (Outer Himalayas - the youngest, lowest foothills composed of unconsolidated river gravel).

Q232
MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
Why are the Northern Plains agriculturally the most productive region of India?
First Principles Breakdown:

Productivity is a function of edaphic (soil) and hydraulic superiority. The Northern Plains are a massive geological depression filled by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems. Over millennia, these rivers eroded the Himalayas and deposited trillions of tons of fine, nutrient-dense Alluvium.
This yields three absolute advantages:
1. Fertility: The soil requires minimal synthetic inputs.
2. Hydrology: The porous alluvium holds an immense, easily accessible groundwater aquifer, complemented by perennial glacial river flow.
3. Topography: Absolute flatness allows frictionless mechanization (tractors) and continuous gravity-fed canal irrigation grids.

Q233
EasyConceptual Descriptive
7. India: Geographical Features
Distinguish between Khadar and Bhangar alluvium.
Glassbox Logic:

These represent the chronological stratigraphy of river deposits.
Khadar: The 'New' alluvium. Located directly within the active floodplains. It is replenished annually by monsoon floods, resulting in fine, light-colored, hyper-fertile soil requiring minimal fertilizers.
Bhangar: The 'Old' alluvium. Located on elevated river terraces sitting safely above the modern flood line. Because it is older and heavily leached, it is darker, less fertile, and heavily contaminated with hard, calcareous nodules known as kankar.

Q234
MediumWhy/How Analytical
7. India: Geographical Features
Why is the Peninsular Plateau the oldest landmass of India? What minerals are found here?
First Principles Breakdown:

The Peninsular Plateau is a primary fragment of the primordial Gondwanaland supercontinent. Unlike the young, actively crumpling Himalayas, this plateau is a dead, highly stable 'shield' constructed entirely from ancient, hard igneous and metamorphic rocks (granites and basalts) that have never been submerged under oceans since the Earth's early crust cooled.
Mineral Wealth: The intense ancient volcanic and tectonic heat crystallized massive concentrations of metallic ores. It is the absolute treasury of India, holding near-monopolies on Iron Ore, Manganese, Bauxite, Copper, and the massive Gondwana Coal reserves (Chota Nagpur plateau).

Q235
MediumConceptual Descriptive
7. India: Geographical Features
Compare the Western Ghats and Eastern Ghats – height, continuity, rivers, biodiversity.
Glassbox Logic:

Height: Western Ghats are towering ($\sim 1,500\text{m}$), whereas Eastern Ghats are severely eroded and low ($\sim 600\text{m}$).
Continuity: Western Ghats form an unbroken, continuous wall (crossable only via specific passes like Thal/Bhor Ghat). Eastern Ghats are highly discontinuous, shattered into disjointed blocks by rivers.
Rivers: Western Ghats act as the primary water divide, birthing massive east-flowing rivers (Godavari, Krishna). The Eastern Ghats are the passive victims of these rivers, which violently cleave through them to reach the sea.
Biodiversity: The unbroken altitude of the Western Ghats intercepts the full force of the SW Monsoon, engineering hyper-dense tropical evergreen rainforests and an unparalleled global biodiversity hotspot, vastly eclipsing the drier Eastern Ghats.
