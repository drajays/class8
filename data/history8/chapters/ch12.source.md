The establishment of the Indian National Congress marked the beginning of an organised political movement by Indians to express their dissatisfaction with the nature of British rule in India. The Indian National Congress, with delegates from different parts of the country, became the mouthpiece for the politically aware Indians.

##### THE MODERATES

When the Indian National Congress was founded in 1885, it was dominated by leaders who have been described as moderates. They believed in the benevolent nature of British rule. They felt that the Indians were still not ready to govern themselves. They firmly believed that the British could be persuaded to introduce reforms for the benefit of Indians.

Many of the moderate leaders came from educated middle class background, well-versed in Western thoughts and ideas. These leaders were teachers, lawyers, journalists and industrialists. The popular

##### Method of Approach

The moderates did not believe in the use of violence. They had faith in the British sense of fair play and justice. They therefore adopted the method of prayer, petition and protest.

The moderates presented their requirements through resolutions and petitions. They believed that the government would eventually pass laws to address these grievances.

### Successes and Failures of the Moderates

The moderates have been criticised by many as they did not achieve many of their objectives. They have also been criticised as a ‘microscopic minority’—leaders who represented only a small section of the population, while the masses at large remained unaffected by their activities. Despite the criticisms levelled at the moderates, they achieved the following:

■ Due to the spread of a national consciousness among a wider section of society, people began to believe that they belonged to one nation.

Due to their efforts, more Indians became aware of the concepts of freedom, secularism and democracy.

The moderates also supported social change and spoke about reforming social evils, such as the rigid caste structure, that divided Indian society.

### RADICAL NATIONALISTS

The failure of the moderates to coax the British government into making real policy changes created dissatisfaction within the Congress. A group of Congress members who were impatient with the moderate methods of protest emerged as new leaders of the Congress. Often referred to as the radical nationalists, they played a more dominant role in the Congress activities from 1905 onwards. The radical nationalists felt that the moderate approach had proved ineffective and that more aggressive methods were necessary.

The radical leadership consisted of aggressive leaders such as Lala Lajpat Rai, Bal Gangadhar Tilak and Bipin Chandra Pal. This trio was popularly known as Lal-Bal-Pal.

#### Beliefs of the Radical Nationalists

The radicals had no faith in the British sense of justice and fair play. They felt that the British had no honest intentions of working for the welfare of the Indian people.

They believed that the British rule had done more harm to India than good.

They demanded complete independence or Swaraj and not self-government within the British Empire.

### Method of Approach

The radical method of approach included aggressive and organised political agitation, which involved the masses.

They were willing to make sacrifices, even when it involved the use of violence, if necessary.

#### Achievements

The radicals felt that the economic interest of the British government to monopolise the Indian markets had harmed Indian industries and artisans. Thus, they promoted the use of goods made in India to revive the declining Indian economy.

The radical nationalists broadened the social base of the movement by involving the masses in their activities.

##### PARTITION OF BENGAL

The belief of the radicals in the oppressive nature of the British rule was further strengthened when the government decided to partition Bengal. The British Viceroy of India, Lord Curzon, was responsible for partitioning Bengal. The official reason given by Curzon was that Bengal was a very large province to be administered effectively. Therefore, Bengal needed to be partitioned to enable competent administration. But the real reason for partitioning Bengal was to drive a wedge between the Hindus and Muslims and thereby weaken the national movement. Thus, Bengal was formally partitioned on 16 October 1905 into East Bengal and West Bengal.

### THE SWADESHI AND BOYCOTT MOVEMENTS

Following the partition of Bengal, the radical nationalists launched the Swadeshi and Boycott movements against the British.

The Swadeshi Movement urged people to use only those goods that were produced by Indian manufacturers.

The Boycott Movement asked people to abstain from purchasing British or foreign goods. It also appealed to Indians to refrain from sending their children to British schools and colleges.

As protests against the partition continued, the differences between the radicals and the moderates became so great that the radicals were expelled from the Congress.

Many of the radical nationalists adopted more revolutionary methods to protest against the partition and British injustice.

These radical nationalists were joined by patriotic young men, and at times women, who were willing to employ aggressive methods to force the British to leave India.

#### THE INDIAN COUNCIL ACT, 1909

In 1909, the British government passed the Indian Council Act, popularly known as the Morley-Minto Reforms. It was named after the Viceroy, Lord Minto and the Secretary of State, John Morley.

Keeping in mind the assurances given to the Muslim League, Lord Minto granted separate electorates. This proved beneficial not only for the Muslims, but also for the British, as it sought to keep the Hindus and the Muslims divided. According to the system of separate electorates, the Hindus could vote only for Hindus and the Muslims could vote only for Muslims.

### TRENDS IN THE NATIONALIST MOVEMENT TILL 1916

In 1916, the Muslim League changed its objective from loyalty to the British rule to demand for self-government within the British Empire. The Muslims of India were disappointed over the British support of the abolition of the Ottoman Empire. The sultan of the Ottoman Empire was also the caliph—the religious head of the worldwide Muslim community. The Muslims perceived this decision as disrespectful of their religious sentiments, and their faith in the British government was shaken.

With this common objective, both the Congress and the Muslim League held their annual session at Lucknow in 1916, and the famous Lucknow Pact was signed. The Lucknow Pact was important for the following reasons:

The Hindus and Muslims were united once again after the partition of Bengal.

The radical nationalists were brought back within the fold of the Congress, thus strengthening the national movement.

Another important event in the history of the Indian national movement was the foundation of the Home Rule League.

It was organised by Annie Besant and Bal Gangadhar Tilak in 1916.

The movement was started to demand Swaraj or self-government.

It succeeded in arousing the masses, revived the Swadeshi spirit and forced the British government to make certain administrative changes.

Nautanki is a folk operatic $ ^{*} $ theatre form, performed in northern parts of India. Central to the performance is a percussion instrument, such as a drum, that is beaten to gather the audience together and at opportune moments in the play.

With the help of your drama teacher, write a nautanki on the events leading up to the Swadeshi and Boycott Movements. Choose rhyming words for your dialogues. Conclude the nautanki showing the various ways in which protests were carried out against the British in these movements. Enact this as a street play in your school campus.

*operative: related to a drama in which participants often sing the dialogues accompanied by music
