In the 18th century, Bengal was considered the richest of all the Indian provinces. This prosperous and fertile land attracted traders from many European countries. Trading companies from Holland, France and England sent representatives to establish their respective trading settlements in Bengal.

In 1717, the Mughal emperor Farrukhsiyar issued a royal order or farman that granted the English East India Company (EEIC) the right to conduct duty-free trade in Bengal. This meant that the British could import and export goods to and from Bengal without paying any taxes to the government. They also had the right to issue dastaks or passes for the free movement of their goods. These favourable developments made the British settlement the largest and most prosperous of the European settlements. Its officials also amassed great wealth.

During the time of the Nawab of Bengal, Alivardi Khan, the British had established and fortified their factories in Bengal.

Alivardi Khan was succeeded by his grandson, Siraj-ud-Daulah. As soon as Siraj-ud-Daulah came to power, he ordered the British to pay taxes. The British refused to obey the order. This annoyed the Nawab. The Nawab's conflict with the British led to the Battle of Plassey, which was a landmark event in the history of Bengal.
