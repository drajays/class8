M ohandas Karamchand Gandhi, popularly known as Mahatma Gandhi or Gandhiji, was born on 2 October 1869 at Porbandar in Gujarat. After his early education in India, he went to England to study law. Thereafter, in 1893, he went to South Africa to practise as a barrister. In South Africa, Gandhi was shocked and enraged at the racial discrimination and oppression meted out by the British colonists to the non-White people. He decided to fight against such injustice.

During Gandhi's stay in South Africa, he evolved the concept of satyagraha (devotion to the truth), based on the principles of truth and non-violence—a concept that he later applied to the Indian national movement.

##### THE CONCEPT OF SATYAGRAHA

Gandhi believed that a person should firmly believe in truth and non-violence and resist evil to follow the principles of satyagraha. A satyagrahi must be fearless and strong and oppose evil actions. He must be willing to make sacrifices and accept suffering.

During the fight for India's freedom from the British rule, Gandhi followed certain methods of protest based on satyagraha. They were as follows:

Peaceful demonstrations

Defiance of unjust British laws

Boycott of British goods and services

Non-payment of oppressive taxes

Promotion of the swadeshi spirit through the use of  $ \underline{\text{charkha}} $ and homespun khadi

##### Early Experiments with

##### Satyagraha

■ Gandhi's first experiment with satyagraha in India was at Champaran (1917) in Bihar. Here he took up the cause of the indigo cultivators against the planters. He launched a movement which forced the government to reduce the rent the Indian peasants were required to pay to the European planters.

After his success at Champaran, Gandhi took up the cause of the peasants of

Kheda in Gujarat in 1918. Gandhi was successful in ensuring the suspension of tax collection for a year because the crops had failed.

In 1918, Gandhi also fought for the textile mill workers of Ahmedabad. He was successful in raising the wages of the workers.

With these early experiments in satyagraha, Gandhi won over popular leaders of the national movement. In Bihar, Dr Rajendra Prasad, and in Gujarat, Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, became enthusiastic and staunch supporters of Gandhi's methods of protest.

##### GANDHI'S VISIONS

Mahatma Gandhi’s emphasis on Hindu–Muslim unity and social justice was another key aspect of his methods for achieving India’s independence.

##### Hindu-Muslim Unity

Gandhi wrote, ‘Indian culture... is neither Hindu, Islamic nor any other, wholly. It is a fusion of all.’ According to him, the path to freedom in India lay in Hindu–Muslim unity and he worked tirelessly towards it.

##### Social Justice

Mahatma Gandhi believed in justice for all—including the poor and downtrodden. He fought to improve the condition of the lower castes and the attitude towards women. He called the people of the lower castes the Harijans or 'the children of God'.

After Gandhi returned to India in 1915, he studied the condition of the Indian national movement closely. He realised that Hindu-Muslim relations were strained and the lower classes were barely a part of the movement. He toured the entire country and came to the conclusion that without the support of the masses, no struggle for independence could be successful. So, he organised regional movements with peasants and workers to create a popular base for the national movement.

### THE NON-COOPERATION MOVEMENT (1920–22)

The Non-cooperation Movement was one of the earliest attempts by Gandhi to bring together all Indians and force the British to grant self-government to India. The causes for the launch of this movement are explained below.

##### Rowlatt Act

The Rowlatt Act was passed in March 1919. It was also known as the Black Act as it curtailed the freedom of the Indian people. The clauses of the Act were as follows:

To carry out arrests without a warrant

To conduct the trials of certain political cases without a jury

Imprisonment for an indefinite period

This act created discontentment and anger against the injustice of the British rule. The government, unable to control the situation, used severely repressive methods. At this point, on 6 April 1919, Gandhi called for a day of national fasting, meetings and suspension of work as an act of satyagraha to protest against the Rowlatt Act.

### Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

On 13 April 1919, a public meeting was called at Jallianwala Bagh in Amritsar to protest against the measures of the Rowlatt Act. The meeting was peaceful and the people were unarmed. However, General Dyer, the British military commander, had issued an order banning all public meetings and hartals. He surrounded the Bagh with his troops, blocking its only exit, and opened fire on the gathering. There was no warning, and the shooting continued till the troops ran out of ammunition. Hundreds were killed and thousands wounded.

This massacre of innocent people was condemned by all Indians. There were severe protests all over the country. Rabindranath Tagore gave up his knighthood in protest. Martial law was declared in Punjab. Gandhi lost all faith in the British government's claim to fair play and justice. He urged people not to believe or cooperate with such a government.

After the Jallianwala Bagh Massacre, Gandhi launched the Non-cooperation Movement in 1920.

The methods of resistance adopted by Gandhi were as follows:

Boycott of legislative councils, law courts and government schools and colleges

Surrender of titles and honorary posts awarded by the British

Promotion of Hindu-Muslim unity

Promotion of swadeshi goods and the boycott of foreign goods

Boycott of government functions and elections

The use of khadi gained popularity among all people, including the upper classes. The charkha, or the spinning wheel, became the symbol of the Non-cooperation Movement.

The Non-cooperation Movement was the first large-scale mass movement organised by Gandhi. Many sections of society were drawn into it. The Congress was transformed from a ‘microscopic minority’ into a mass-based party. Hindu–Muslim unity was negotiated. The movement, by supporting the Khilafat Movement, included Muslims in the fight for freedom from British domination.

When the movement was at its peak, violence erupted in 1922 at Chauri Chaura—a small town in the district of Gorakhpur in Uttar Pradesh. The police fired at a peaceful procession. The angry mob retaliated and burnt down the police station, killing some policemen. Gandhi disapproved this violence and immediately called off the movement.

##### Did You Know?

In 1919, Muslims in British India launched a pan-Islamic political protest campaign. Known as the Khilafat Movement, it was led by the Ali brothers—Muhammad Ali and Shaukat Ali. The Ali brothers began the movement to protest against the abolition of the office of the Caliph in Turkey.

### THE CIVIL DISOBEDIENCE MOVEMENT (1930–34)

The Civil Disobedience Movement launched by Gandhi and the Congress in 1930 also followed the method of satyagraha. The events led to this movement are discussed as follows.

### Reaction against the Simon Commission

In 1927, a commission headed by Sir John Simon was sent by the British government to review its functioning in India. It came to be known as the Simon Commission.

From the outset, the Simon Commission was boycotted by the Indians, mainly because it was an all-White commission. It had no Indian member to represent India. The recommendations of the Simon Commission were also not acceptable to the national leaders.

##### Poorna Swaraj Resolution

The Congress held its annual session at Lahore in December 1929. Jawaharlal Nehru was the President at this session of the Congress. In this session, the resolution of Poorna Swaraj, or complete independence, was adopted. The decision to boycott the First Round Table Conference in London and declare 26 January 1930 as Independence Day for India were also taken. The Indian tricolour was adopted and unfurled, and people all over the country took the pledge of freedom. The Congress also decided to launch a Civil Disobedience Movement under Gandhi's leadership.

In March 1930, Gandhi launched the Civil Disobedience Movement with the Salt Satyagraha. The British government had established its monopoly over salt, a necessary item in every household. Indians were forced to pay tax on salt and were not allowed to manufacture it.

##### Dandi March

Gandhi undertook the historic and famous Dandi March to protest against the salt laws. He marched from the Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi—a village on the coast of Gujarat—to break these laws. He was joined by thousands of people on the way.

The Civil Disobedience Movement became an all-India mass movement. Strikes, hartals, boycott of government offices, schools and colleges brought life to a standstill. People defied the British authorities fearlessly. They refused to pay taxes or buy foreign goods. A significant aspect of the movement was that it was truly non-violent in nature. Women too participated in this movement.

##### Government Repression

The government came down with severe repressive measures, but this did not deter the people. Important leaders, including Jawaharlal Nehru and Gandhi, were arrested, thousands of Indians were imprisoned and

hundreds were injured and even killed in lathi-charge and police firing. Nationalist feelings and aspirations were strong at this point. The British realised that tangible reforms were necessary to pacify the Indians.

##### Gandhi-Irwin Pact

Realising that a dialogue with the Indian leaders had become necessary, the Viceroy Lord Irwin signed a pact with Gandhi, known as the Gandhi-Irwin Pact. According to this pact, Gandhi agreed to suspend the Civil Disobedience Movement and attend the Second Round Table Conference. Lord Irwin, on his part, agreed to release all prisoners and permit people to manufacture salt.

### The Second Round Table Conference and Relevance of Civil Disobedience Movement

The Second Round Table Conference was held between September and December 1931. Gandhi was chosen as the sole representative of the Congress. At the Conference, there was no discussion of granting the rights of self-governance to Indians. Gandhi returned empty-handed and relaunched the Civil Disobedience Movement.

The Civil Disobedience Movement made it clear to the British that certain tangible constitutional reforms would have to be granted to the Indians. The failure of the Round Table Conferences had further strengthened this idea. As a result, the British passed the Government of India Act in 1935.

##### Government of India Act, 1935

Political activity in India intensified after the Government of India Act, 1935. Elections were held in 1937. While both the Congress and the Muslim League decided to contest the elections, the Indian National Congress won by a huge margin.

When World War II broke out in 1939, Britain realised the need for Indian support to fight the war. Nationalist leaders of India were willing to help Britain, provided India was made independent. This led to a deadlock between the British government and the Indian nationalist leaders.

### INDIAN NATIONAL ARMY AND SUBHAS CHANDRA BOSE

Subhas Chandra Bose, also known as Netaji, was a very important leader of the Indian national movement. He entered politics at an early age and aggressively fought for Swaraj. He became the Congress President twice—1938 and 1939. When differences arose between him and Gandhi, Bose resigned from the presidentship of the Congress.

Subhas Chandra Bose formed the Forward Bloc in 1939. The main objectives of the Forward Bloc were to get freedom from the British Rule in the immediate future and to promote national unity. When the British declared war against the Axis powers on India's behalf, Bose organised a mass protest in Calcutta. He was imprisoned and then kept under house arrest, from where he escaped to Berlin and later to Japan.

#### KEY WORDS

Axis powers: the nations, namely Germany, Japan and Italy, that fought in World War II against the Allied powers constituting France, Poland, Great Britain, the USA and the Soviet Union

Civil Disobedience Movement: launched in 1930 to protest against unjust laws; involved disobeying laws and boycotting British goods, services and institutions

Dandi March: symbolised the launch of the Civil Disobedience Movement—Gandhi marched from Sabarmati to Dandi to break the salt laws

.Jans: means the 'children of God'; the name given by Gandhi to the untouchable castes Hartal: a strike in protest of something

Indian Independence Act: passed in July 1947 by the British Parliament to end the British rule in India

Khadi: an Indian homespun cotton cloth

Non-cooperation Movement: launched by Gandhi in 1920 for the attainment of Swaraj through peaceful means

Planters: Europeans during the British Raj who often owned indigo factories and forced peasants to grow indigo

Salt laws: included a number of acts passed by the British government which made production and possession of salt by Indians illegal; only the government could collect and manufacture salt at the salt depots in India

Satyagraha: a movement started by Gandhi based on the principles of truth and non-violence
