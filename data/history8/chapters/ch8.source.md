### ECONOMIC IMPACT OF THE BRITISH RULE

Towards the end of the 18th century, the EEIC was no longer a mere trading company. It had been transformed into a formidable colonial power controlling most of India. In order to retain control over their colony, the British established a system of administration for the maintenance of law and order, as well as for internal security.

Indian agriculture received special attention from the EEIC. This was primarily because the main source of the state income was land revenue. Moreover, it was the aim of the British government to establish India as an agricultural supplier of cheap raw materials to industries in England. The Company tried various experiments to maximise the land revenue, regardless of its oppressive impact on Indian peasants. Some of these land revenue policies were as follows:

■ Lord Cornwallis introduced the Permanent Settlement as a system of revenue collection in 1793 in Bengal, Bihar and Orissa.

The Ryotwari Settlement was introduced in Bombay and Madras Presidencies.

The Mahalwari Settlement was introduced in the northern part of the country, mainly in Uttar Pradesh and Punjab, where it had devastating effects.

##### Impact of these settlements

The revenue settlements were exploitative and oppressive. The following points describe the negative effects of these revenue policies on agriculture in India:

Land became a saleable commodity, which could be bought, sold or mortgaged.

The zamindar, in order to collect more money, pressurised the peasants to pay more tax. Thus, he could set aside the extra revenue for himself.

Peasants were forced to pay revenue in times of famine as well. If they failed to pay the revenue on time, they were evicted from their land.

The zamindars as well as the British were completely indifferent to the suffering and exploitation of the peasants.

Frequent eviction from their land led to the growth of a class of landless peasants.

The peasants were forced to borrow money from the moneylenders in order to pay the revenue, even if their crops failed. Moneylenders added to the misery of the peasants. They lent money at high interest rates, which the poor peasants were unable to repay and fell into a cycle of debt.

Over cultivation of land depleted the fertility of the soil and resulted in the occurrence of regular famines.

#### Impact on Industries

Indian industries also suffered a great setback under the British colonial rule. The British government, in the early years of the 18th century, imposed heavy duties on Indian textile imports to Britain. On the other hand, British machine-made goods entered India duty-free or at a nominal cost. The introduction of such a policy of one-way free trade made Indian goods very costly. It resulted in a significant decrease in sale for the Indian artisans and loss of their livelihoods.

##### Transport and Communication

The British realised that to consolidate their position in India, the development of proper communication facilities was necessary. They built an elaborate network of roads, canals and railway lines to connect all major cities, ports and agricultural centres. Metalled roads were built, and the railway system was introduced.

##### Benefits of Improved Transport System

The improved transport system ensured more efficient governance for the British. They could quickly transport troops and rapidly communicate to quell revolts and other disturbances.

It became easier to transfer raw materials and finished goods for the promotion of trade and commerce.

Although the transport and communication system was established to further British imperialist designs, it benefited Indian merchants as well. They now had an efficient system through which they could export raw materials such as cotton, jute and indigo and import finished goods from Europe. The merchants made a lot of profit and became very wealthy.

This in turn infused a sense of unity and resulted in the growth of nationalism—a development which in due course brought an end to the British rule in India.

#### Economic Exploitation of India

The British government drained the Indian economy and exploited it to the maximum. The exploitation took the following forms:

The British bought raw materials at cheap prices and sold their manufactured goods at high prices, flooding the Indian markets with their products. This led to the ruin of small-scale cottage industries.

The peasants were forced to pay high taxes, leading to increase in the poverty level and discontent. Therefore, a number of peasant revolts and uprisings took place.

The workers were paid low wages. The British made no effort to improve industry or agriculture in India.

India's wealth was being systematically drained away to England.

### ANGLICIST-ORIENTALIST DEBATE

In the early 19th century, the British government passed the Charter Act of 1813, whereby the Company was required to spend one lakh rupees annually on the education of Indians. The Charter Act of 1813 sparked off a debate on education across England as people argued about what policy should be implemented to educate the people of India. One group argued that the traditional learning should be imparted in classical and regional Indian languages. These were the Orientalists. On the other hand, the Anglicists, led by Thomas Macaulay, insisted that the medium of instruction should be English and the education imparted should be Western.

### Wood's Despatch on Education

Charles Wood, the President of the Board of Control, was a British government official responsible for overseeing the EEIC.

In 1854, he sent a despatch to the Governor-General of India, Lord Dalhousie.

Known as the Wood's Despatch, it laid the foundation on which the modern system of education in India developed.

It was declared that the aim of the British government was the promotion of Western education.

Wood prepared a comprehensive report with several recommendations in order to improve the system of education in India.

For higher education, the medium of instruction was to be English.

Almost all the proposals in the Wood's Despatch were diligently implemented.

English medium schools were set up.

Later, girls' schools were set up and brought under the control of the Government of India. Several grants

were also provided for the promotion of women's education.

Three universities for higher education were established in Calcutta, Bombay and Madras in 1857.

#### Arrange a Bird Corner in your Balcony/Garden

#### Materials required:

In the present times, we can continue to appreciate and gain knowledge about the biodiverse flora and fauna of our country. These activities will help you to do that.

2 shallow, wide-mouthed clay pots from the local potter

<div style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://pplines-online.bj.bcebos.com/deploy/official/paddleocr/pp-ocr-vl-16-online//2e711e65-a95b-4bb5-a33b-b61413db8a62/markdown_3/imgs/img_in_image_box_947_1133_1102_1282.jpg?authorization=bce-auth-v1%2FALTAKDN8mY5KlNI7zaRpLmOqrw%2F2026-06-10T19%3A44%3A36Z%2F-1%2F%2F01429a4a13cc0122cbaa5736480a32716bcafe1230c9a42cb79447f0c3332cd4" alt="Image" width="13%" /></div>


Clean water

Bird seeds

## Method:

Arrange these in a relatively quiet section of your balcony or garden. Keep your movement around this area to the minimum. Fill one clay pot with fresh water and the other one with bird seeds.

##### Over a period of time:

Observe the types of birds that visit your bird corner.

• Photograph as many of these birds as possible.

You can sketch these birds like the British did too.

Learn the names of the birds that visit—both local and botanical names.

Study the habits of each type of bird. What sound does it make? Which time of the day does it come? Which month of the year does it come?
