In the 18th century, Bengal was considered the richest of all the Indian provinces. This prosperous and fertile land attracted traders from many European countries. Trading companies from Holland, France and England sent representatives to establish their respective trading settlements in Bengal.

In 1717, the Mughal emperor Farrukhsiyar issued a royal order or farman that granted the English East India Company (EEIC) the right to conduct duty-free trade in Bengal. This meant that the British could import and export goods to and from Bengal without paying any taxes to the government. They also had the right to issue dastaks or passes for the free movement of their goods. These favourable developments made the British settlement the largest and most prosperous of the European settlements. Its officials also amassed great wealth.

During the time of the Nawab of Bengal, Alivardi Khan, the British had established and fortified their factories in Bengal.

Alivardi Khan was succeeded by his grandson, Siraj-ud-Daulah. As soon as Siraj-ud-Daulah came to power, he ordered the British to pay taxes. The British refused to obey the order. This annoyed the Nawab. The Nawab's conflict with the British led to the Battle of Plassey, which was a landmark event in the history of Bengal.

##### BATTLE OF PLASSEY (1757)

The Battle of Plassey marked the beginning of the end of the rule of Nawabs in Bengal.

##### Causes

The following are the causes for the outbreak of the Battle of Plassey:

#### Fortifications

The French had established their settlement in Chandernagore. Anticipating war with the French, the British began to fortify their settlement in Calcutta without the Nawab's permission. Siraj-ud-Daulah was enraged at this direct challenge to his power. The Nawab asked the British and the French to pull down their fortifications. While the French agreed, the British refused. This led to a direct conflict between the Nawab and the British.

#### Capture of Calcutta

In 1756, Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah marched towards Calcutta with a large army and captured Fort William, which was a British stronghold. However, Robert Clive soon recaptured Calcutta. Consequently, the Treaty of Alinagar was signed between the British and Siraj-ud-Daulah. According to the terms of the treaty, the Nawab restored trading rights and the city of Calcutta to the British. Rights to fortify Calcutta were also given to the British. However, the British were not satisfied and they wanted to place a puppet ruler on the throne of Bengal to suit their expansionist designs. The British under Clive, with the support of the former subedar of Bengal, Mir Jafar, presented a set of unreasonable demands to the Nawab, which made the battle inevitable.

#### The Battle

The Battle of Plassey was fought in June 1757 at Plassey (near Murshidabad). The British forces advanced under the leadership of Robert Clive, while Siraj-ud-Daulah led an army of about 50,000 men.

A large part of the Nawab's army was under the command of Mir Jafar, which did not participate in the battle. The Nawab himself left the battlefield after the death of his army commander, Mir Madan. The disheartened army was easily defeated. Later, Siraj-ud-Daulah was captured and put to death, and Mir Jafar was declared the Nawab of Bengal. The English gained control over Bengal by installing Mir Jafar as the puppet ruler.

#### Did You Know?

Jagat Seth was a rich Marwari businessman from Murshidabad during the rule of Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah. Described by the official historian of the EEIC, Roben Orme, as the greatest banker in the known world, Jagat Seth conspired with Mir Jafar and Robert Clive against Siraj-ud-Daulah.

##### Results of the Battle

The British victory at the Battle of Plassey had far-reaching consequences.

The EEIC acquired the undisputed right to trade in Bengal, Bihar and Orissa.

The Company was given zamindari rights over 24 parganas.

Mir Jafar paid a huge sum of money to the Company in return.

##### Importance of the Battle

The Battle of Plassey is considered to be a turning point in the history of India due to the following reasons:

It paved the way for the establishment of the British rule in India.

The Nawab of Bengal became a puppet in the hands of the British. The British transformed themselves from a trading company into a political power.

The vast resources of Bengal passed on to British hands. These resources were used to finance the Third Carnatic War and other battles for expansion in India.

##### Aftermath of the Battle

■ Mir Jafar was a Nawab only in name. He had responsibilities, but no power.

After the Battle of Plassey, the EEIC relentlessly plundered the seemingly inexhaustible resources and wealth of Bengal.

The Company directors ordered Bengal to pay for the expenses of administering the Bombay and Madras Presidencies.

■ Mir Jafar realised that it was impossible to meet the growing demands of the EEIC. He sought help from the Dutch East India Company and asked them to intervene.

■ The Dutch sent a force, but the British forces defeated them at Chinsurah—the Dutch stronghold.

The British soon deposed Mir Jafar and appointed Mir Qasim (Mir Jafar's son-in-law) as the new Nawab.

##### Reforms by Mir Qasim

Mir Qasim was an ambitious ruler. On becoming the Nawab, he immediately granted the zamindari rights over Burdwan, Midnapore and Chittagong to the British. Mir Qasim also undertook certain reforms to reorganise Bengal's administration, which was in a chaotic state.

■ Mir Qasim shifted his capital from Murshidabad to Monghyr, far from the British control.

He hired French officials to train and discipline the Bengal army. The new army was modern and well-equipped.

■ To prevent the misuse of duty-free trading privileges by the British, Mir Qasim abolished all duties on internal trade for Indians as well. This affected the British monopoly in trade and enraged them.

A series of wars broke out between Nawab Mir Qasim and the British. The most important amongst these wars was the Battle of Buxar.

### DUAL GOVERNMENT IN BENGAL (1765-72)

After the victories at Plassey and Buxar, the British became the real rulers of Bengal. A new  $ \underline{\text{administrative system was established}} $ in 1765. It was known as the  $ \underline{\text{Dual Government}} $ in Bengal.

### Drawbacks of the Dual Government

According to this system, the English enjoyed all the privileges of being a ruler, but had no responsibility. They had complete military power and the right to collect the revenue in Bengal and use it as they wished.

The Nawab, on the other hand, had to look after the administration, and law and order.

According to the system of Dual Government, the Nawab of Bengal also had to disband most of his army,

A small amount of revenue was given to the Nawab to run the civil administration.

☐ Actual administrative powers, and most importantly, the collection of revenue was controlled by the British.

##### Effects of the Dual Government

Such a system adversely affected the life of people in Bengal in the following ways:

Corruption of the officials who collected taxes grew.

Misery of the people, particularly peasants, increased.

The British were only interested in amassing wealth and did not bother about the welfare of the people. This was evident when the Great Famine of 1770 broke out in Bengal, in which more than one-third of the population perished.

#### End of the Dual Government (1772)

The defects of the Dual Government soon became visible to the British Parliament.

The system of administration and the economy of Bengal collapsed.

☐ The Company now directly took over the control of Bengal's administration.

☐ The Company appointed Warren Hastings as the new governor of Bengal.

By the Regulating Act of 1773, Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General of India.

Warren Hastings introduced various reforms and extended British territory far beyond Bengal.

Hastings is credited with laying the foundations of the British colonial rule in Bengal and introducing an organised system of government in Bengal.
