1. Matter (62 Questions — 31 HR)

Topics: Kinetic theory, states of matter, change of state, density, latent heat, evaporation, sublimation, diffusion, pressure.

  1. Q1. Define matter and name the three states in which it commonly exists. [Easy]
    Ans: Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass. Three states: solid, liquid, gas.
  2. Q2. [HR] Why does a gas fill the entire container while a solid retains its shape? Explain using the kinetic theory of matter. [Medium]
    Ans: Gas particles have negligible intermolecular forces and move randomly at high speeds, allowing them to spread throughout. Solid particles have strong intermolecular forces and vibrate in fixed positions, maintaining shape.
  3. Q3. Which state of matter has: (a) definite shape and volume, (b) definite volume but no shape, (c) neither shape nor volume? [Easy]
    Ans: (a) Solid, (b) Liquid, (c) Gas.
  4. Q4. [HR] How does the kinetic theory explain why evaporation causes cooling? Use the concept of energy distribution among molecules. [Medium]
    Ans: Molecules have varying kinetic energies. High-energy molecules escape during evaporation, lowering the average kinetic energy of remaining molecules, which reduces temperature.
  5. Q5. Name the process by which: (i) solid → liquid, (ii) liquid → gas, (iii) gas → liquid, (iv) liquid → solid. [Easy]
    Ans: (i) Melting/fusion, (ii) Vaporisation/boiling, (iii) Condensation/liquefaction, (iv) Freezing/solidification.
  6. Q6. [HR] Why does the temperature remain constant during the melting of ice even though heat is continuously supplied? [Medium]
    Ans: The supplied heat is used as latent heat of fusion to overcome intermolecular forces and change state, not to increase kinetic energy. Hence temperature remains constant.
  7. Q7. What is latent heat? State its SI unit. [Easy]
    Ans: Latent heat is the heat required to change the state of a substance without temperature change. SI unit: J/kg.
  8. Q8. [HR] How would the rate of evaporation of water change if: (a) humidity increases, (b) surface area increases, (c) temperature increases? Justify each with molecular reasoning. [Hard]
    Ans: (a) Decreases — more vapour already present reduces concentration gradient. (b) Increases — more surface molecules exposed to air. (c) Increases — more molecules gain escape energy; kinetic energy distribution shifts right.
  9. Q9. Define sublimation and give two examples of substances that sublime. [Easy]
    Ans: Sublimation is direct conversion from solid to gas. Examples: Camphor, Naphthalene, Dry ice (CO₂).
  10. Q10. [HR] Why does naphthalene disappear over time without leaving any liquid residue? Explain the energy changes involved at the molecular level. [Medium]
    Ans: Naphthalene sublimes; solid molecules absorb latent heat from surroundings, gain enough energy to overcome intermolecular forces directly into the vapour phase without passing through liquid.
  11. Q11. A solid substance is heated until it becomes gas. Draw a heating curve and label melting point and boiling point. [Medium]
    Ans: Temperature vs time graph with plateaus at melting point (solid→liquid) and boiling point (liquid→gas).
  12. Q12. [HR] How does increasing pressure affect the melting point of ice and the boiling point of water? Explain using Le Chatelier’s principle of equilibrium. [Hard]
    Ans: Ice (less dense) melts at lower temperature under pressure. Water boils at higher temperature under pressure because increased pressure opposes expansion; molecules need more energy to escape.
  13. Q13. Calculate the density of a wooden block of mass 240 g and volume 300 cm³. [Easy]
    Ans: Density = mass/volume = 240/300 = 0.8 g/cm³ = 800 kg/m³.
  14. Q14. [HR] Why does a cotton plug in a bottle containing volatile perfume loosen over time? Explain in terms of diffusion and pressure. [Medium]
    Ans: Perfume vapour diffuses into the air between cotton fibres. Gas pressure inside the plug increases as more vapour molecules enter, pushing fibres apart and loosening the plug.
  15. Q15. What is diffusion? Arrange solids, liquids, and gases in increasing order of rate of diffusion. [Easy]
    Ans: Diffusion is the intermixing of particles of different substances. Rate: solids < liquids < gases.
  16. Q16. [HR] Why does diffusion occur faster in hot water than in cold water? Relate this to molecular kinetic energy and collision frequency. [Medium]
    Ans: Higher temperature increases kinetic energy, raising both molecular speed and collision frequency. This increases the mixing rate and widens the concentration gradient.
  17. Q17. State Boyle’s law and Charles’s law. [Easy]
    Ans: Boyle’s law: P ∝ 1/V (at constant T). Charles’s law: V ∝ T (at constant P).
  18. Q18. [HR] How does a gas exert pressure on the walls of its container? Draw a molecular picture to explain. [Medium]
    Ans: Gas molecules move randomly and collide elastically with walls. Each collision exerts a tiny force; billions of collisions per second produce a steady macroscopic pressure.
  19. Q19. A gas occupies 2 L at 1 atm. If pressure is doubled at constant temperature, what is the new volume? [Easy]
    Ans: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → 1×2 = 2×V₂ → V₂ = 1 L.
  20. Q20. [HR] Why does a balloon burst when heated but shrink when placed in a freezer? Explain using kinetic theory and gas laws. [Medium]
    Ans: Heating increases molecular kinetic energy; collisions become more frequent and forceful, increasing internal pressure until material fails. Cooling reduces kinetic energy and pressure, allowing elastic material to contract.
  21. Q21. Define melting point and boiling point. [Easy]
    Ans: Melting point: temperature at which solid becomes liquid. Boiling point: temperature at which liquid boils at 1 atm.
  22. Q22. [HR] How does the addition of impurities affect the melting point of ice? Justify with an everyday example. [Medium]
    Ans: Impurities lower the melting point by disrupting the crystal lattice. Example: Salt is spread on roads in winter to melt ice at temperatures below 0°C.
  23. Q23. What is the normal melting point and boiling point of water? [Easy]
    Ans: 0°C and 100°C at 1 atm.
  24. Q24. [HR] Why does water boil at a lower temperature on a mountain top? How does this affect cooking time? Explain. [Medium]
    Ans: Atmospheric pressure is lower at high altitudes; boiling occurs when vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure. Lower boiling point means less heat transfer per unit time to food, increasing cooking time.
  25. Q25. Define the term 'latent heat of vaporisation' and give its value for water. [Easy]
    Ans: Heat required to convert 1 kg of liquid to vapour at constant temperature. For water: 2260 kJ/kg.
  26. Q26. [HR] Why does steam at 100°C cause more severe burns than boiling water at 100°C? Use the concept of latent heat. [Medium]
    Ans: Steam carries additional latent heat of vaporisation (2260 kJ/kg). When it condenses on skin, it releases this extra heat, delivering more total energy than water at the same temperature.
  27. Q27. An ice cube of mass 50 g at 0°C melts to water at 0°C. How much heat is absorbed? (L_f = 334 J/g) [Medium]
    Ans: Q = mL = 50 × 334 = 16,700 J = 16.7 kJ.
  28. Q28. [HR] How does evaporation differ from boiling? Construct a comparative table covering temperature, surface, bubble formation, and energy source. [Hard]
    Ans: Evaporation: occurs at any temperature, surface phenomenon, no bubbles, absorbs heat from surroundings. Boiling: occurs at fixed temperature, bulk phenomenon, bubbles form, heat supplied externally.
  29. Q29. Why does a liquid cool when it evaporates? [Easy]
    Ans: High-energy molecules escape; average kinetic energy of remaining molecules decreases, lowering temperature.
  30. Q30. [HR] Why does perspiration help regulate body temperature? Explain the role of humidity in limiting this cooling mechanism. [Medium]
    Ans: Sweat evaporates from skin, absorbing latent heat from the body. High humidity reduces the concentration gradient between skin and air, slowing evaporation and reducing cooling efficiency.
  31. Q31. Name three factors that increase the rate of evaporation. [Easy]
    Ans: Increase in temperature, increase in surface area, decrease in humidity, increase in wind speed.
  32. Q32. [HR] Why does a wet cloth hung in a windy place dry faster than in still air? Use the concept of vapour pressure gradient. [Medium]
    Ans: Wind removes the layer of saturated air near the cloth, maintaining a steep vapour pressure gradient between cloth surface and surrounding air. This increases the net molecular flux away from the surface.
  33. Q33. What is the effect of pressure on the boiling point of a liquid? [Easy]
    Ans: Increasing pressure raises the boiling point; decreasing pressure lowers it.
  34. Q34. [HR] Why is a pressure cooker useful for cooking at high altitudes? How does the pressure-temperature relationship affect food chemistry? [Hard]
    Ans: It increases internal pressure, raising boiling point above 100°C. Higher temperature increases the rate of starch gelatinisation and protein denaturation, reducing cooking time.
  35. Q35. Define the terms: (a) condensation, (b) freezing, (c) deposition. [Easy]
    Ans: (a) Gas to liquid, (b) Liquid to solid, (c) Gas to solid (reverse of sublimation).
  36. Q36. [HR] Why does frost form directly on cold surfaces without going through the liquid phase? Explain the thermodynamic conditions required. [Hard]
    Ans: When surface temperature is below the frost point (deposition point), water vapour molecules lose kinetic energy directly to the solid lattice. Low temperature and low partial pressure of water vapour favour deposition over condensation.
  37. Q37. A beaker contains 200 g of water at 20°C. How much heat is needed to raise its temperature to 60°C? (Specific heat capacity of water = 4.2 J/g°C) [Medium]
    Ans: Q = mcΔT = 200 × 4.2 × 40 = 33,600 J = 33.6 kJ.
  38. Q38. [HR] Why does a piece of iron feel colder than a piece of wood at the same temperature on a winter morning? Explain in terms of thermal conductivity and heat transfer. [Medium]
    Ans: Iron has higher thermal conductivity. It draws heat away from your skin faster than wood, causing a larger rate of heat loss from your body, which your nerves interpret as colder.
  39. Q39. What is the SI unit of density? Convert 2.5 g/cm³ into kg/m³. [Easy]
    Ans: kg/m³. 2.5 g/cm³ = 2.5 × 1000 = 2500 kg/m³.
  40. Q40. [HR] Why does an iron needle float on water but an iron ship floats despite higher density? Relate to buoyancy and displaced volume. [Hard]
    Ans: A needle does not displace enough water to generate buoyant force equal to its weight. A ship displaces a huge volume of water; by Archimedes’ principle, the upthrust equals the weight of displaced water, which can balance the ship’s weight.
  41. Q41. State the principle of conservation of mass during a physical change. [Easy]
    Ans: Mass remains constant during physical changes; no new substance is formed.
  42. Q42. [HR] How does compressing a gas change its density? If you compress a gas isothermally to half its volume, how does molecular collision frequency with the walls change? [Hard]
    Ans: Density doubles because same mass occupies half volume. Collision frequency doubles because molecules have half the distance to travel between wall impacts and same speed.
  43. Q43. What is the density of water at 4°C? Why is this value significant? [Medium]
    Ans: 1000 kg/m³ (1 g/cm³). It is the maximum density of water; water expands both above and below this temperature.
  44. Q44. [HR] Why does water expand on freezing while most substances contract? Explain the hydrogen bonding and anomalous structure of ice. [Hard]
    Ans: Ice forms an open hexagonal crystal lattice due to hydrogen bonding. This structure is less dense than the liquid state where molecules are closer. Most substances lack this directional bonding and simply pack more efficiently in the solid state.
  45. Q45. Define the term 'vapour pressure' and state what happens when it equals atmospheric pressure. [Medium]
    Ans: Vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by a vapour in equilibrium with its liquid. When it equals atmospheric pressure, the liquid boils.
  46. Q46. [HR] Why does a volatile liquid (like ether) evaporate faster than water at the same temperature? Relate this to intermolecular forces and latent heat. [Hard]
    Ans: Ether has weaker intermolecular forces (van der Waals) and lower latent heat of vaporisation. Less energy is needed for molecules to escape the liquid, so the evaporation rate is higher.
  47. Q47. Calculate the mass of 500 cm³ of aluminium if its density is 2.7 g/cm³. [Easy]
    Ans: Mass = density × volume = 2.7 × 500 = 1350 g = 1.35 kg.
  48. Q48. [HR] How would you experimentally prove that particles of matter are in continuous motion? Design a simple demonstration using diffusion. [Medium]
    Ans: Place a crystal of potassium permanganate in a beaker of water. Colour spreads even without stirring, showing random molecular motion (Brownian motion and diffusion).
  49. Q49. What is Brownian motion? Who discovered it? [Easy]
    Ans: Random zig-zag motion of particles suspended in a fluid. Discovered by Robert Brown.
  50. Q50. [HR] Why does Brownian motion provide evidence for the kinetic theory of matter? What would happen to Brownian motion if the temperature were reduced to absolute zero? [Hard]
    Ans: The random motion of visible particles is caused by unbalanced collisions with invisible fluid molecules. At absolute zero, molecular motion ceases, so Brownian motion would stop (third law of thermodynamics).
  51. Q51. Define the term 'specific heat capacity'. [Easy]
    Ans: The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C.
  52. Q52. [HR] Why does water have a very high specific heat capacity compared to metals? How does this property stabilise Earth’s climate? [Hard]
    Ans: Water has extensive hydrogen bonding requiring more energy to increase molecular motion. Oceans absorb large amounts of solar heat in summer and release it in winter, moderating temperature extremes.
  53. Q53. Name the change of state for: dry ice left in the open, wax melting in a candle, dew forming on grass. [Easy]
    Ans: Sublimation, melting, condensation.
  54. Q54. [HR] Why does a refrigerator cool the food inside but heat the room? Explain using energy conservation and the refrigeration cycle. [Hard]
    Ans: A refrigerator transfers heat from inside to outside using work. The heat expelled into the room equals heat removed from inside plus work done by the compressor. Net effect: room temperature increases.
  55. Q55. What is the triple point of water? Why is it unique? [Hard]
    Ans: 0.01°C at 611.7 Pa. It is the unique temperature and pressure where all three phases coexist in equilibrium.
  56. Q56. [HR] Why does a hot solid object cool faster in air than in a vacuum? Compare the roles of conduction, convection, and radiation. [Hard]
    Ans: In vacuum, only radiation transfers heat. In air, conduction and convection add additional heat transfer paths, increasing the total cooling rate.
  57. Q57. Define the term 'relative density' and state its formula. [Easy]
    Ans: Ratio of density of a substance to density of water at 4°C. RD = density of substance / density of water.
  58. Q58. [HR] How can you use the concept of relative density to determine if a crown is made of pure gold? Describe the method and the physics behind it. [Hard]
    Ans: Measure mass and volume by water displacement. Calculate density and compare to gold density (19.3 g/cm³). Archimedes used this principle. If density is lower, the crown contains less dense metals.
  59. Q59. Which has more heat: 1 kg of water at 50°C or 1 kg of iron at 100°C? (c_water = 4200 J/kg°C, c_iron = 450 J/kg°C) [Medium]
    Ans: Heat in water = 4200 × 50 = 210,000 J. Heat in iron = 450 × 100 = 45,000 J. Water has more heat.
  60. Q60. [HR] Why does a swimming pool feel cold even on a warm day while the air feels warm? Use concepts of thermal conductivity, specific heat, and convection. [Hard]
    Ans: Water has high specific heat and thermal conductivity, drawing heat from skin rapidly. Convection currents also continually replace warmed water with cooler water. Air has low conductivity and convection is weaker.
  61. Q61. State the difference between heat and temperature. [Easy]
    Ans: Heat is total internal energy in transit; temperature is average kinetic energy of molecules.
  62. Q62. [HR] How does the kinetic energy of particles change during a phase change? Why does the potential energy change significantly while kinetic energy remains constant? [Hard]
    Ans: During phase change, temperature is constant so average kinetic energy is constant. Energy input increases potential energy by increasing intermolecular separation and overcoming forces, changing the degree of freedom.

2. Physical Quantities & Measurement (62 Questions — 31 HR)

Topics: SI units, measurement of length, mass, time, density, area, volume, significant figures, errors, instruments (vernier, screw gauge), graphs, speed, velocity.

  1. Q63. Define physical quantity and distinguish between fundamental and derived quantities. [Easy]
    Ans: A physical quantity is a property measurable by an instrument. Fundamental: independent (mass, length, time). Derived: expressed in fundamental (speed, density, force).
  2. Q64. [HR] Why did the SI system replace the CGS and FPS systems? Discuss advantages in terms of decimalisation, universality, and scientific communication. [Medium]
    Ans: SI is coherent, decimal-based, and universally standardised. Powers of 10 simplify conversions. It removes ambiguity in international trade and research.
  3. Q65. Name the SI unit of: (a) length, (b) mass, (c) time, (d) electric current, (e) temperature. [Easy]
    Ans: (a) metre (m), (b) kilogram (kg), (c) second (s), (d) ampere (A), (e) kelvin (K).
  4. Q66. [HR] How does the definition of the metre based on the speed of light improve precision over the original platinum-iridium bar definition? [Medium]
    Ans: Speed of light is a fundamental constant of nature, invariant and reproducible everywhere. The bar was subject to thermal expansion, wear, and required physical access.
  5. Q67. Convert: (a) 5 km to m, (b) 250 g to kg, (c) 2.5 hours to seconds. [Easy]
    Ans: (a) 5000 m, (b) 0.25 kg, (c) 9000 s.
  6. Q68. [HR] Why is the kilogram the only base SI unit still defined by a physical artefact (until recently)? Why was the Kibble balance redefinition important? [Hard]
    Ans: Mass was difficult to link to atomic constants. The Kibble balance relates mass to Planck’s constant via electromagnetic force, making the definition universal and independent of a physical object.
  7. Q69. What is the least count of a metre scale? How do you calculate it? [Easy]
    Ans: 1 mm or 0.1 cm. Least count = smallest division on the scale.
  8. Q70. [HR] How does parallax error arise when reading a scale? Describe two practical ways to eliminate it. [Medium]
    Ans: Parallax occurs when the eye is not directly above the mark. Solutions: (1) Use a mirror strip behind the pointer (in meters) to align eye perpendicular, (2) Repeat measurement from opposite sides and average.
  9. Q71. A vernier caliper has 10 divisions on the vernier scale coinciding with 9 divisions on the main scale (1 mm each). What is the least count? [Medium]
    Ans: LC = 1 MSD - 1 VSD = 1 - 0.9 = 0.1 mm = 0.01 cm.
  10. Q72. [HR] Why does a vernier caliper provide more accurate readings than a metre scale? Explain the mathematical principle of vernier coincidence. [Medium]
    Ans: A vernier subdivides the smallest main scale division. The n-th vernier division matches (n-1) main divisions, allowing interpolation. The least count is the difference between 1 MSD and 1 VSD, giving precision to 0.1 mm or better.
  11. Q73. A screw gauge has a pitch of 0.5 mm and 50 divisions on the circular scale. Find its least count. [Medium]
    Ans: LC = pitch / no. of divisions = 0.5 / 50 = 0.01 mm.
  12. Q74. [HR] Why is the screw gauge called a micrometer? How does the ratchet mechanism prevent zero error and excessive pressure on the object? [Hard]
    Ans: It measures in micrometres (0.01 mm). The ratchet slips at a preset torque, ensuring consistent measuring pressure and preventing elastic deformation of soft objects or backlash error.
  13. Q75. Define zero error and zero correction with examples. [Easy]
    Ans: Zero error: instrument reading when jaws are closed. Zero correction = - zero error. Positive zero error: zero of vernier is right of main scale zero.
  14. Q76. [HR] Why does a screw gauge develop backlash error after prolonged use? How does reversing the direction of rotation before taking the reading minimise it? [Hard]
    Ans: Wear in the screw thread creates play. Backlash occurs when the screw moves without advancing the nut. Always approaching the measurement from the same direction (by rotating past the object and then backing up) ensures the screw thread is loaded on the same flank.
  15. Q77. The main scale reading of a vernier caliper is 3.2 cm and the 4th vernier division coincides. If LC = 0.01 cm, what is the total reading? [Easy]
    Ans: Total = 3.2 + (4 × 0.01) = 3.24 cm.
  16. Q78. [HR] How would you measure the internal diameter of a test tube using a vernier caliper? Why is the internal jaw design crucial? [Medium]
    Ans: Use the upper internal jaws. They are designed to spring outward against the inner walls, ensuring contact at the true internal diameter rather than a chord. The zero of the vernier must be aligned to the main scale with proper depth gauge usage.
  17. Q79. What is the principle of a physical balance? State the condition for equilibrium. [Easy]
    Ans: Principle of moments. Equilibrium when sum of clockwise moments equals sum of anticlockwise moments.
  18. Q80. [HR] Why does a physical balance have a rider on the beam? How does moving the rider by 1 cm affect the moment if the rider mass is 10 mg? [Hard]
    Ans: The rider provides small mass adjustments without adding/removing weights. Moment = force × distance. Moving 1 cm changes moment by 10 mg × 1 cm = 10 mg·cm. This allows precise fractional gram measurement.
  19. Q81. Define density and relative density. Write their SI units. [Easy]
    Ans: Density = mass/volume (kg/m³). Relative density = density of substance/density of water (no unit).
  20. Q82. [HR] Why does a hydrometer float higher in a denser liquid? Derive the relationship between immersion depth and liquid density using the flotation principle. [Hard]
    Ans: Floating condition: weight = upthrust = ρ_liquid × V_submerged × g. For constant weight, V_submerged ∝ 1/ρ. Denser liquid means less volume submerged, so the hydrometer floats higher.
  21. Q83. A metal block measures 5 cm × 4 cm × 2 cm and has mass 320 g. Find its density and relative density. [Easy]
    Ans: Volume = 40 cm³. Density = 320/40 = 8 g/cm³. RD = 8 (since water density = 1 g/cm³).
  22. Q84. [HR] How would you experimentally determine the density of an irregularly shaped solid that is soluble in water? Outline the method and reasoning. [Hard]
    Ans: Use a displacement method with a non-reactive, non-dissolving liquid (e.g., kerosene or turpentine). Alternatively, coat the solid with a thin impermeable wax layer and use water displacement. Measure volume by liquid rise in a measuring cylinder.
  23. Q85. What is the volume of 500 g of mercury? (Density of mercury = 13.6 g/cm³) [Easy]
    Ans: V = m/ρ = 500/13.6 ≈ 36.76 cm³.
  24. Q86. [HR] Why does the density of a liquid change with temperature, and why is this a problem for accurate volume measurement? How do laboratories compensate? [Hard]
    Ans: Liquids expand on heating, reducing density. Volume measurements become temperature-dependent. Labs use calibrated vessels at standard temperature (20°C or 27°C) and apply volumetric expansion corrections for precise work.
  25. Q87. Define speed and velocity. Distinguish between them. [Easy]
    Ans: Speed = distance/time (scalar). Velocity = displacement/time (vector). Velocity has direction.
  26. Q88. [HR] Why does a speedometer in a car show speed but not velocity? What additional instrument would be needed to measure velocity? [Medium]
    Ans: Speedometer measures rate of wheel rotation, giving scalar magnitude. To measure velocity, a GPS or compass direction sensor combined with speed is needed to determine the vector component.
  27. Q89. A car travels 120 km in 2 hours and returns in 3 hours. Calculate average speed and average velocity for the whole journey. [Medium]
    Ans: Total distance = 240 km, total time = 5 h. Average speed = 240/5 = 48 km/h. Net displacement = 0, so average velocity = 0.
  28. Q90. [HR] How can average velocity be zero while average speed is non-zero? In what type of motion are both equal? Explain with a real-life example. [Medium]
    Ans: Zero displacement with non-zero distance (round trip). Both are equal only in straight-line motion without reversal. Example: athlete running 400 m on a circular track has zero displacement but 400 m distance.
  29. Q91. What is acceleration? State its SI unit and whether it is a scalar or vector. [Easy]
    Ans: Rate of change of velocity. m/s². Vector.
  30. Q92. [HR] Why does a body thrown upward have negative acceleration (taking upward as positive) even though it is slowing down? Explain the sign convention. [Medium]
    Ans: Acceleration is downward (gravity). If upward is positive, downward acceleration is negative. Negative acceleration means velocity is decreasing in the positive direction (slowing) or increasing in the negative direction (speeding up while falling).
  31. Q93. A body starts from rest and reaches 20 m/s in 4 s. Find acceleration. [Easy]
    Ans: a = (v-u)/t = (20-0)/4 = 5 m/s².
  32. Q94. [HR] How does a position-time graph distinguish between uniform motion, rest, and accelerated motion? What does the slope represent? [Medium]
    Ans: Straight line = uniform motion. Horizontal line = rest. Curved line = accelerated. Slope = velocity.
  33. Q95. Draw a velocity-time graph for a body with constant positive acceleration. [Easy]
    Ans: Straight line with positive slope starting from origin or initial velocity.
  34. Q96. [HR] How is the area under a velocity-time graph related to displacement? Prove this mathematically for uniformly accelerated motion. [Hard]
    Ans: For constant acceleration, area = trapezoid/rectangle/triangle area = (u+v)/2 × t. Since v = u + at, area = ut + ½at² = displacement s.
  35. Q97. What is the significance of significant figures in measurement? [Easy]
    Ans: They indicate the precision of a measurement and the reliability of the last digit.
  36. Q98. [HR] Why does adding a very precise measurement (4.2345 m) to a rough measurement (2.1 m) give a result that should be reported as 6.3 m? Explain the rule of decimal places in addition. [Medium]
    Ans: The result cannot be more precise than the least precise measurement. 2.1 has uncertainty in tenths place, so the sum is limited to tenths place: 6.3 m, not 6.3345 m.
  37. Q99. Express 0.00450 in scientific notation with correct significant figures. [Easy]
    Ans: 4.50 × 10⁻³ (3 significant figures).
  38. Q100. [HR] How does the number of significant figures in a calculated density depend on the precision of mass and volume measurements? If mass is 12.34 g and volume is 2.1 cm³, how many significant figures should the density have? [Hard]
    Ans: In division, the result has the same number of significant figures as the least precise measurement. Volume has 2 sig figs, so density = 5.9 g/cm³ (2 sig figs).
  39. Q101. Define the term 'pitch' in relation to a screw gauge. [Easy]
    Ans: Pitch is the linear distance moved by the screw along the main scale in one complete rotation.
  40. Q102. [HR] Why does a screw with a smaller pitch give more precise measurements? What is the trade-off when using a very fine pitch? [Hard]
    Ans: Smaller pitch means smaller linear advance per rotation, so smaller least count. Trade-off: more rotations needed, increasing wear, backlash, and time. The thread is also mechanically weaker.
  41. Q103. What is a stopwatch? Name the type that can measure up to 0.01 s. [Easy]
    Ans: A device to measure time intervals. Digital stopwatch measures to 0.01 s.
  42. Q104. [HR] How does human reaction time introduce systematic error in stopwatch measurements? Estimate this error and suggest an experimental method to reduce it. [Hard]
    Ans: Reaction time is ~0.2–0.3 s. For short events, this is a large percentage error. Use electronic timing gates, light sensors, or multiple observers averaging to reduce it. Use a longer time interval to reduce relative error.
  43. Q105. A pendulum completes 20 oscillations in 40 s. Calculate the time period. [Easy]
    Ans: T = total time / oscillations = 40/20 = 2 s.
  44. Q106. [HR] Why does a simple pendulum’s time period depend on length but not on mass? Use dimensional analysis to support your answer. [Hard]
    Ans: T = 2π√(L/g). Mass cancels out in the equation of motion because gravitational force (mg) and inertia (m) both scale linearly with mass. Dimensional analysis: [T] = [L]^a [g]^b. Solving gives T ∝ √(L/g), independent of mass dimension.
  45. Q107. Define the terms: (a) uniform speed, (b) variable speed, (c) average speed. [Easy]
    Ans: (a) Equal distances in equal time intervals. (b) Unequal distances in equal intervals. (c) Total distance / total time.
  46. Q108. [HR] Why is the speedometer reading not exactly equal to the instantaneous speed? What mechanical or electronic limitations exist? [Hard]
    Ans: Speedometers average over a short response time (mechanical inertia or electronic sampling delay). Also, tyre wear, pressure changes, and slipping alter the wheel circumference, introducing calibration errors.
  47. Q109. A train covers 240 km at 60 km/h and the next 120 km at 40 km/h. Find the average speed for the whole journey. [Medium]
    Ans: Time 1 = 240/60 = 4 h. Time 2 = 120/40 = 3 h. Average speed = total distance / total time = 360/7 ≈ 51.43 km/h.
  48. Q110. [HR] Why is average speed not the arithmetic mean of individual speeds? Under what special condition does the arithmetic mean become valid? [Hard]

    Ans: An absolute mathematical mandate requiring that every single additive term on both sides of a valid physical equation must possess the exact same fundamental base SI dimensions. You cannot equate mass to time, nor can you add velocity to acceleration.
  28. Q490. [HR] A student postulates a new kinematic formula for distance: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^3$. Utilize rigorous Dimensional Analysis to instantly falsify this mathematical hypothesis without requiring a single numerical experiment. [Medium]
    Ans: We strip the equation to its absolute base dimensions [Length = $L$, Time = $T$].
    LHS: Displacement $s$ has dimensions $[L]$.
    RHS Term 1: $u \cdot t = [LT^{-1}] \times [T] = [L]$. (This term is valid).
    RHS Term 2: $a \cdot t^3 = [LT^{-2}] \times [T^3] = [LT]$.
    The equation attempts to add a term representing pure Distance $[L]$ to a bizarre term representing Distance multiplied by Time $[LT]$. This violates the ironclad law of Dimensional Homogeneity (you cannot mathematically add meters to meter-seconds). The equation is irrefutably false.
  29. Q491. Identify the astronomical phenomenon labeled 'Dark Matter'. [Easy]
    Ans: An invisible, hypothetical form of matter believed to constitute ~85% of the universe's mass. It strictly refuses to interact with electromagnetic radiation (light) but violently exerts gravitational forces on massive galactic structures.
  30. Q492. [HR] Detail the crisis of Galactic Rotation Curves discovered by Vera Rubin. How did the outer rim velocities of spiral galaxies mathematically mandate the existence of the Dark Matter halo? [Hard]
    Ans: Newtonian mechanics and Kepler's laws mathematically dictate that as you move farther away from the massive galactic center, the orbital velocity of outer stars must rapidly decay (drop off) because the gravitational grip weakens ($v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$). Vera Rubin measured the Doppler shifts of spiral galaxies and found a horrifying anomaly: the outer stars were not slowing down. They were flying at blistering, flat velocities far exceeding escape velocity. By all known laws of physics, these galaxies should have instantly ripped themselves apart and dissolved. The only mathematical salvation is that the visible galaxy is embedded in a colossal, invisible 'halo' of immense mass pulling the stars inward—the proof of Dark Matter.
  31. Q493. What is the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)? [Easy]
    Ans: The relic thermal radiation left over from the Big Bang, discovered as a faint, uniform microwave hum permeating the entire observable universe at a temperature of approximately 2.7 Kelvin.
  32. Q494. [HR] The CMB is the "afterglow" of the Big Bang, a cataclysm of unimaginable heat. Why does our radio-telescope telemetry record this radiation as a freezing, ultra-low energy microwave signal rather than blinding, lethal gamma radiation? [Hard]
    Ans: Roughly 380,000 years after the Big Bang (the Epoch of Recombination), the universe cooled enough for electrons to bind to protons, allowing photons to finally fly free. At that instant, the radiation was a blinding, blazing orange-white heat ($\sim 3000$ K). However, the fabric of spacetime has been violently expanding for 13.8 billion years since that flash. As the universe stretches, the photon waves traveling through it are literally stretched apart. This infinite cosmic stretching elongated the ultra-short visible light wavelengths by a factor of 1000, severely dropping their frequency and energy via cosmological redshift, transforming the blistering heat into a freezing, invisible microwave hum.
  33. Q495. Define the 'Null Hypothesis' strictly within the framework of the Scientific Method. [Easy]
    Ans: The default, conservative baseline assumption that there is absolutely no significant relationship, no statistical deviation, or no causal effect between two measured variables in a rigorous scientific experiment.
  34. Q496. [HR] Why is the philosophy of 'Falsifiability', championed by Karl Popper, the absolute bedrock of empirical physics? Why is an unfalsifiable theory scientifically useless? [Medium]
    Ans: Popper declared that a theory cannot be proven infinitely true by a million supporting observations, because a single contradictory observation tomorrow will destroy it. Therefore, a genuine scientific theory must be 'Falsifiable'—it must boldly make highly specific, risky predictions that can be subjected to empirical testing and potentially proven completely wrong. A theory that is so vague or untestable that it can dynamically accommodate absolutely any outcome (like "an invisible, undetectable dragon lives in my garage") yields zero predictive power and is exiled from the realm of hard science.
  35. Q497. What is the 'Arrow of Time' in modern physics? [Easy]
    Ans: The unidirectional, asymmetrical forward progression of time from the past to the future, establishing strict causality, fundamentally driven by the relentless thermodynamic increase of cosmic disorder.
  36. Q498. [HR] Look closely at Newton's kinematic equations and Maxwell's electrodynamics. They are completely 'Time-Symmetric' (they function flawlessly if time runs backward). What singular law of physics destroys this symmetry and enforces the relentless forward march of time? [Hard]
    Ans: The Second Law of Thermodynamics. While a video of a billiard ball bouncing off a cushion looks physically perfectly valid in reverse (Newtonian symmetry), a video of a shattered teacup spontaneously reassembling itself and leaping onto a table is instantly recognizable as impossible. The Second Law strictly mandates that the absolute Entropy (chaotic disorder and degraded heat) of an isolated system must relentlessly increase. This unidirectional climb from highly-ordered states to disorganized, high-entropy sludge provides the singular, irrefutable mathematical vector that we perceive as the Arrow of Time.
  37. Q499. Define the concept of a 'Thought Experiment' (Gedankenexperiment) and name Einstein's most famous example. [Easy]
    Ans: A highly rigorous, logical mental simulation of a physical scenario that is currently too technologically impossible to physically execute in a lab. Example: Einstein imagining himself chasing a beam of light at velocity $c$ to deduce Special Relativity.
  38. Q500. [HR] Congratulations on reaching the capstone. Synthesize your knowledge: If you drop a massive iron anvil and a single feather simultaneously inside a colossal, perfect vacuum chamber on Earth, justify precisely why they strike the floor at the exact same millisecond, obliterating Aristotle's 2000-year-old dogma. [Hard]
    Ans: Aristotle believed heavier objects fell faster because gravity pulled them harder. He was only half right. The immense gravitational weight vector ($W = Mg$) does pull the massive anvil down with exponentially greater force than the feather. However, Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$) demands we also account for Inertia. The anvil's massive mass also grants it immense Inertial Resistance to acceleration. The massive gravitational pull is perfectly, mathematically canceled out by the massive inertial sluggishness. Setting the forces equal ($Mg = Ma$), the mass variable ($M$) flawlessly cancels out of the algebra entirely ($g = a$). Thus, in a perfect vacuum completely devoid of aerodynamic fluid drag, the intrinsic acceleration of gravity is universally independent of mass. The feather and the anvil accelerate at an identical 9.8 m/s² and impact simultaneously, a beautiful victory of invariant mathematics over flawed human intuition.
