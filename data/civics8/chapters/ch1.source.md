India is a parliamentary democracy, where the supreme power resides with the people of the country. The Indian Constitution has declared India to be a Union of States. In India, power is distributed between the central and the state governments.

There are three organs of the government of India.

Legislature—makes laws

Judiciary—interprets the laws and checks the working of the legislature and executive

Executive—executes the laws

##### WHO MAKES OUR LAWS?

<div style="text-align: center;"><img src="https://pplines-online.bj.bcebos.com/deploy/official/paddleocr/pp-ocr-vl-16-online//776bf468-5b4b-4208-ae97-d56e49380a38/markdown_1/imgs/img_in_image_box_0_1110_75_1293.jpg?authorization=bce-auth-v1%2FALTAKDN8mY5KlNI7zaRpLmOqrw%2F2026-06-10T19%3A44%3A44Z%2F-1%2F%2Fa83d9f8e16faa82668ac03d88040e182c6572854997208e719f9b6622d5cf894" alt="Image" width="6%" /></div>


Legislature is a part of the government, which makes and amends laws. The Indian Parliament, known as the Sansad, is the law-making body at the central level. The state legislatures make laws at the state level. The legislature makes laws on subjects given in three lists—Union List, State List and Concurrent List (Refer to the table given on the next page).

Union List: It includes subjects, such as banking and defence, on which only the Centre can legislate.

State List: The state legislatures make laws on subjects given in the State List. Subjects such as agriculture and public health are the prerogative of the states.

Concurrent List: Subjects such as economic and social planning, electricity and education, which can be concerns for both the Centre and the states, are provided in this list. However, if there is a conflict between the Centre and a state over a subject in the Concurrent List, the central law will prevail.

##### THE UNION PARLIAMENT

The Parliament is a representative body, which can make or change the country's laws. The Indian Parliament consists of the Lok Sabha, the Rajya Sabha and the President.

##### Lok Sabha

The Lok Sabha is also known as the House of the People or the Lower House.

##### Composition

The maximum strength of the Lok Sabha is 552.

Up to 530 members can be represented from the states, and not more than 20 members shall represent the Union Territories.

Two members of the Anglo-Indian community may be nominated by the President to the Lok Sabha, if she/he feels that this community is not adequately represented in the House.

The allotment of seats to various states is on the basis of population.

Seats are also reserved for the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes in the Lok Sabha.

##### Manner of Election

In India, every citizen who is above the age of 18 years has been given the right to vote without being discriminated on the basis of caste, religion and sex, under Universal Adult Suffrage.

### Qualifications for Members of the Lok Sabha

Must be a citizen of India

Must not be less than 25 years of age

Must be of sound mind

Must not be bankrupt or convicted in any criminal case

##### Term of Office

The term of the Lok Sabha is five years.

The President has the power to dissolve the Lok Sabha before its term ends.

The term of the House can be extended during the Proclamation of Emergency.

##### Sessions of the House

A session means a period during which the House meets to conduct business.

The two sessions of the Parliament are summoned by the President. However, 6 months must not lapse between two sessions.

The Speaker is the presiding officer of the Lok Sabha.

Lok Sabha conducts three sessions in a year, namely, the Budget Session, the Monsoon Session and the Winter Session.

##### Rajya Sabha

The Rajya Sabha is also known as the Upper House or the Council of States. The presiding officer of the Rajya Sabha is the Vice President of India. She/He is the ex-officio Chairperson of the Rajya Sabha.

##### Composition

The maximum strength of the Rajya Sabha is 250 members.

The members are either nominated or elected.

The President nominates 12 members, from persons having special knowledge, or practical experience in matters, such as science, arts, literature or social services.

The Rajya Sabha has 238 elected members who are the representatives of states and union territories.

Seats in the Rajya Sabha are allocated according to the population of each state.

##### Manner of Election

The members of the Rajya Sabha are indirectly elected by the system of proportional representation, by the means of a single transferable vote system.

Qualifications and disqualifications for the members of the Rajya Sabha are the same as that of the members of the Lok Sabha. However, a candidate wishing to be a member of the Rajya Sabha must not be less than 30 years of age.

##### Term of Office

The Rajya Sabha is a permanent house.

One-third of the members retire every two years.

A member elected for a full term serves for a period of six years.

### FUNCTIONS OF THE UNION PARLIAMENT

A bill is introduced in either House of the Parliament, if it is an ordinary bill. A bill is a draft of a proposed law presented to the Parliament for discussion. However, a money bill can only be introduced in the Lok Sabha. A bill goes through three stages of reading. In the first stage, the bill is introduced in the House. In the second stage, voting on the bill takes place after the debate. Then the bill is sent to the other House, where the same procedure is followed. In case of a money bill, the Rajya Sabha is given only 14 days to consider the bill. In the third stage, the bill is sent to the President for his assent. The President can either sign the bill or send it back for reconsideration to the House, where the bill was presented. When the President signs the bill, the bill becomes an Act.

##### President

The President or Rashtrapati is the head of the State. She/He exercises executive powers either directly or through officers. The President is also the first citizen of the country.

#### Qualifications for the Post of President

Must be a citizen of India

Must be at least 35 years of age

Should not hold an office of profit under the government—Centre, state or local level

Must be qualified to become a member of the Lok Sabha

##### Election and Removal

The President is elected indirectly by an electoral college consisting of the Members of Parliament (MPs), and the Members of Legislative Assemblies (MLAs) of the states.

The President is elected for a period of five years. She/He can be re-elected for another term.

The President's office can fall vacant under the following instances:

Expiry of the term

On death of the President

☐ Resignation

□ Impeachment

##### Impeachment of President

Impeachment is a procedure outlined in our Constitution, by which the Parliament can remove the President. The President is impeached if she/he violates the Constitution, or is guilty of treason or corruption.

#### Powers and Functions of the President

The powers of the President can be grouped under the following heads:

##### Executive Powers

The President appoints the Prime Minister, who is the leader of the single largest party in the Lok Sabha or the leader of the coalition that wins the elections.

The President also appoints the Council of Ministers on the advice of the Prime Minister.

The President appoints and removes the high dignitaries of the states. They include  $ \underline{\text{Attorney General}} $, Comptroller

and Auditor General of India, Governors of states, ambassadors, members of the Finance Commission and Union Public Service Commission, Chief Election Commissioner, and Chief Commissioner's of Union Territories.

Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts are also appointed by the President.

##### Military Powers

The President is the  $ \underline{\text{Supreme}} $ Commander of the Defence Forces.

The President has the power to declare war and conclude peace.

All important treaties are made in the name of the President.

The  $ \underline{\text{President}} $ represents (India) in international conferences.

##### Legislative Powers

The  $ \underline{\text{President summons}} $ the Parliament, and can dissolve the Lok Sabha.

No bill can become an Act without the assent of the President. In case of an ordinary bill, she/he can send it back with her/his recommendation to the House where the bill originated. In case of a money bill, she/he has to sign it in the first instance.

The President nominates two members of the Anglo-Indian community in the Lok Sabha. Moreover, 12 members to the Rajya Sabha from among persons having special knowledge in literature, arts, science and social service are also nominated by the President.

The President can issue ordinances when the Parliament is not in session. Ordinances are temporary laws passed by the President. They have the force of law, and are valid for not more than six weeks after the Parliament meets.

##### Financial Powers

The annual budget is presented in the name of the  $ \underline{\text{President}} $.

All money bills are introduced in the Parliament with the President's consent.

The Contingency Fund is at the disposal of the President. She/He advances money in case of unforeseen expenditure.

##### Judicial Powers

The President has the  $ \underline{\text{power}} $ to grant pardon, or reduce the sentence of the guilty. She/He can also pardon a death sentence.

She/He is not answerable to any court of law, other than being impeached by the Parliament.

##### Emergency Powers

An emergency is a sudden or unforeseen situation demanding immediate action. The President cannot declare an emergency without the approval of the Parliament. She/He proclaims emergency on the following grounds:

If security of the country is threatened by external aggression or armed rebellion, the situation is called a national emergency.

If the  $ \uwave{\text{administrative}} $ machinery of a state breaks down, President's  $ \uwave{\text{Rule}} $ is imposed in the state.

If the financial stability of the country is  $ \underline{\text{threatened}} $, financial emergency is declared.

#### Discretionary Powers

When no party wins an absolute majority in the Lok Sabha, the President can appoint the Prime Minister of her/his choice—a person, who she/he thinks, can command a majority in the House.

##### Vice President

The Vice President is elected indirectly by an electoral college consisting only of members from both Houses of Parliament. The term of office of Vice President is 5 years.

She/He is removed by a resolution of the Rajya Sabha, passed by a majority of its members. It has to be agreed by the Lok Sabha.

The Vice President acts on behalf of the President under the following circumstances:

If the President resigns

If the President dies in  $ \underline{\text{office}} $

If the President is  $ \underline{\text{indisposed owing to}} $ illness or  $ \underline{\text{absence}} $, or any other reason

### Powers and Functions of the Vice President

The Vice President, is the ex-officio Chairperson of the Rajya Sabha. She/He regulates debates and proceedings of the House. She/He decides the admissibility of resolutions and questions in the House in case of grave disorder. She/He heads various committees and coordinates their working.

#### Prime Minister

While the President is the  $ \underline{\text{nominal or

constitutional head}} $ of the State, the Prime Minister is the head of the  $ \underline{\text{government}} $. The Prime Minister is the pivot on which the whole constitutional machinery runs.

### Appointment of Prime Minister and Council of Ministers

The Prime Minister is appointed by the President. In actual practice, the President is bound to appoint the leader of the majority party that wins the Lok Sabha elections as the Prime Minister. Other ministers are appointed by the President on the advice of the Prime Minister.

If no party gets an absolute majority, then two or more parties may join together and form the government. This is known as a coalition government.

##### Term of Office

The term of office for the Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers is usually five years. However, it can even be dissolved earlier if a no-confidence motion is passed against the ministry.

##### Categories of Ministers

There are three categories of ministers—Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State and Deputy Ministers. The Cabinet Ministers form the inner core of the council. They hold important portfolios, such as finance, railways, defence, home and foreign affairs. The Ministers of State and the Deputy Ministers do not attend Cabinet meetings.

#### THE JUDICIARY

After the legislature and executive, the judiciary is the third branch or pillar of the Indian democratic setup. The Constitution of India has provided us with a single unified judiciary.

##### The Supreme Court

The Supreme Court is the guardian of the Constitution. It can interpret the Constitution and declare any law null and void, if it is not in accordance with the Constitution. The Supreme Court interprets all the laws of the union as well as the states. The Subordinate Courts, the

District Courts and the High Courts of the states all come below the Supreme Court. The Supreme Court of India is located in New Delhi.

##### Composition

Headed by the Chief Justice, the Supreme Court of India comprises a maximum of 30 other judges.

##### Did You Know?

H.J. Kania was the first Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of India. He served on this post from 1950 to 1951.

##### Appointment and Removal

The President of India appoints the Chief Justice and other judges of the Supreme Court. While appointing the judges of the Supreme Court, judges of the Supreme Court and the High Courts in the states are to be consulted. While appointing the Chief Justice, the President holds consultation with the judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts in the states.

A judge of the Supreme Court may resign from the office by writing to the President. A judge may also be removed from office through impeachment by the Parliament, on the grounds of proven misbehaviour or incapacity. The procedure to remove a judge from office is called impeachment.

##### Term of Office

Once appointed, a judge of the Supreme Court holds office till she/he attains the age of 65 years.

#### Some of the Qualifications

Must be a citizen of India

Must have been a judge of a High Court for at least 5 years or must have been an advocate of a High Court for at least 10 years

Must be, in the opinion of the President,

a distinguished jurist

Jurisdiction and Powers of the Supreme Court

##### Original Jurisdiction

Original jurisdiction extends to those cases, which the Supreme Court has the authority to hear and decide in the first instance. These are as follows:

Cases between the Government of India and the states

■ Cases between the Government of India and any state vs other states

Cases between two or more states

■ Cases involving the violation of Fundamental Rights. In such cases, the Supreme Court issues writs to enforce the Fundamental Rights.

##### Appellate Jurisdiction

Appellate cases are those which are appeals against the judgements of High Courts. Appellate cases can be the following:

Constitutional cases

Civil cases

☑ Criminal cases

##### Advisory Jurisdiction

The Supreme Court can give advice to the President on constitutional issues, when asked by the President. The President, however, is not bound to follow its advice.

##### Other Powers

Court of Record: The Supreme Court is a Court of Record. The judgements

are recorded, and are in the  $ \underline{\text{nature of precedents}} $. This means that the  $ \underline{\text{High Court}} $ and other courts are bound to give a similar decision in a similar case.

Revisory: The Supreme Court has the power to review any judgement. This provision shows that human judgement is fallible, and the court may rectify something that was misjudged.

Judicial Review: This can be defined as the power of the Supreme Court to examine the laws passed by the legislature, and the orders issued by the executive. The aim is to ensure whether they are permitted by the Constitution.

##### High Court

The High Court is at the apex of the judiciary in the State. Each High Court has jurisdiction over one or more states and union territories. All union territories fall within the jurisdiction of the High Court of a neighbouring state. Delhi has its own High Court.

##### Composition

Each High Court has a Chief Justice, and other judges as the President may appoint. The number of judges is not fixed. It varies from state to state.

##### Appointment and Removal

The Chief Justice of the High Court is appointed by the President of India. He does this in consultation with the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court and the governor of the state concerned. The appointments of other judges are made in the same manner. However, the Chief Justice of the High Court shall also be consulted for the same.

The Chief Justice and other judges can be removed by the President's order after impeachment. The Parliament commences impeachment if it finds them guilty of proven misbehaviour or incapacity.

#### Some Qualifications

Must be a citizen of India

Should have held a judicial office in India for at least 10 years or should have been an advocate of the High Court for at least 10 years

##### Term of Office

Once appointed, a judge of the High Court serves till the age of 62. If a judge wishes, she/he can resign earlier or can be removed by the President. The same process as per which the judges of the Supreme Court are removed is followed.

#### Powers of the High Court

#### Original Jurisdiction

Original jurisdiction refers to those cases which the High Court hears and decides for the first time. These include:

Disputes concerning the Fundamental Rights

Cases involving election petitions

Cases regarding wills, marriages, divorces and contempt of court.

#### Appellate Jurisdiction

The High Court  $ \underline{\text{hears}} $ appeals, against judgements passed by the subordinate courts.

##### Subordinate Courts

Under each High Court, there are several lower courts known as subordinate courts. These courts are under the jurisdiction of the High Court.

##### District Court

The District Court and the court of the Sessions Judge are the highest courts in the district. In some places, the District Judge also acts as the Sessions Judge.

##### Writs

The Supreme Court and the High Courts have the power to issue writs for the enforcement of the Fundamental Rights. Writs are written commands issued by a court or other legal authority to act or abstain from doing illegal acts. The examples of writs are heabeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari.

Democracy is a form of government in which people choose leaders and representatives by voting and everyone is treated equally and has equal rights. India is a representative democracy. It means that in our country, eligible people vote for others to carry out the business of government. We elect them to be our voice.

##### Activity 1

You have been assigned the role of a representative for your constituency. You are getting ready for the election campaign, which will start soon. Write and give a speech about what you would offer if ‘elected’.

#### Activity 2

Role-play: After you are elected as an MP for your constituency, you have been asked to design a new park to celebrate the 100th year celebration of Independence Day in India. You can decide how big the park should be and what will be included in it. You have an unlimited budget. Perform a role-play using dialogues and actions based on the key principles of working in a representative democracy:

• Your whole group discusses the park design and facilities that it should offer.

• The group then votes and selects two most suitable people to work on the design.

• The two elected representatives can consult the others for advice at any time.

• The final project can be inaugurated by the Prime Minister and declared open for the people.
